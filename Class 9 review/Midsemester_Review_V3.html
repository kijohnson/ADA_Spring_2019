What is advanced data analysis?

  • Envisioned in part as teaching students how NOT TO DO bad science (or how TO DO good science) from the analyst perspective
  • In our program, first year classes (biostats, epi methods, ALM) provide an introduction to different models used in statistical analysis without getting much into all the different components that go into producing a solid statistical evaluation of a public health problem.
  • AND of course all along the way much experience is obtained with statisitcal software (R in this case) and data management

What consitutes good science? (Dr. Johnson’s top 5)

  1. A reproducible work flow so that 10 years from now or even just 6 months from now you or someone else can reproduce the results from a publication or report, exactly. What information do you need to provide to help ensure reproducibility?

  2. Knowledge of the main statistical models used to analyze public health problems–when they are appropriate, when they are not appropriate, what you should do to assess model fit/diagnostics.

  3. Adjusting for covariates using DAGs. We SHOULD NOT throw the kitchen sink in the model. This is a tricky business and we a systematic way of covariate selection based on logic and practical issues (not p-values) is needed.

  4. Robustness checks–when uncertain of what the best model is, do it multiple ways, also do sensitivity analyses to see if conclusions change based on your CHOICE of how to model the data.

  5. Accurate communication of findings

CLASS FIVE: Logistic regression

  • Dependent variable: Binary variable
  • Independent variable: Categorical and continuous
  • Measure of Association: Odds ratio (OR) (exp of \(\beta\) from the model)
  • Model assumptions: Linearity between independent variable and log odds of outcome variable, no multicollinearity, and no influential data
  • What does the model predict?
    • The probability that an observation falls into one category given the independent variables
    • The log odds that an observation falls into one category given independent variables
  • Model things to check: - log-likelihood - deviance = -2 X log-likelihood - null deviance = model without any predictors - residual deviance = model with predictors - likelihood ratio test = null deviance - residual deviance = chi-square with df from base model - df from full model (i.e. with additional predictor(s))

*Note to compare models you need to make sure they include the same number of observations (nested models)

  • Sensitivity and specificity of the model (model fit–how well does the model do at classifying people correctly)
  • How do we interpret the results? The log odds of being in one category increases by n for every one unit increase in x (or compared to reference group if categorical). The odds of being in one category increases by n times for every one unit increase in x (or compared to reference group if categorical).
  • Example of age and BMI categories’ association with diabetes status (1 = diabetic). For every one year increase in age, the odds of being diabetic increases by 2% (OR = 1.02). Individuals who are overweight have 2 times the odds of having diabetes compared to those who are underweight (OR = 2).

Multinomial logistic regression

  • Dependent variable: Categorical variable with J levels
  • Independent variable: Continuous or categorical
  • Measure of Association: Odds ratio (OR) (exp of \(\beta\) from the model)
  • Model assumptions: Linearity between independent variable and log odds of outcome variable, no multicollinearity, and no influential data
  • What does the model predict?
    • The log odds that an observation falls into category J given independent variables
  • Model fit: Can compare two models using the likelihood ratio test
  • How do we interpret the results? The log odds of being in category J is n higher/lower in the exposed group vs. the reference group. The odds of being in category J in the exposed group is n times that of the reference group.
  • Example of high, medium, low steps walked per week in association with high, medium, low BMI. The odds of reporting walking a high number of steps per week is 2 times higher in those with low BMI vs. high BMI (OR = 2).

Ordinal logistic regression

  • Dependent variable: ordered categorical variable with J levels
  • Independent variable: Continuous or categorical
  • Measure of Assocation: Odds Ratio (exp of \(\beta\) from the model)
  • Model assumptions: proportional odds assumption (can be tested by comparing deviances between multinomial and ordinal model, from pg. 272 Fox and Weisberg “An R companion to Applied Regression 2nd edition”). Example below.

#read in the data
Breast_SEER_Class6 <- read_delim("Breast_SEER_Class6.txt", "\t", escape_double = FALSE, trim_ws = TRUE)

#assign variable names that are easy to read
names(Breast_SEER_Class6)<-c("Age", "Race", "Sex", "Diagnosis_year", "Stage", "First", "PatientID", "Insur", "Marital", "Poverty") 

#data management
Data <- Breast_SEER_Class6 %>%
   mutate(Male_cat=factor(Sex),
         First_cat=factor(First),
         Age_num=as.numeric(gsub("([0-9]+).*$", "\\1",Age)),
         #Non-Black as reference
         Black_cat=ifelse(Race=="White"
                         |Race=="Other (American Indian/AK Native, Asian/Pacific Islander)", 0,
                            ifelse(Race=="Black", 1, NA)),
         Black_cat=factor(Black_cat, levels=0:1, labels = c("Non-Black", "Black")),
         #Stage I as reference
         Stage_cat=ifelse(Stage == "IA" | Stage == "IB", 0,
                         ifelse(Stage == "IIA" | Stage == "IIB",1,
                                ifelse(Stage == "IIIA" |Stage == "IIIA" 
                                       |Stage == "IIIC" |Stage =="IIINOS",2,
                                       ifelse(Stage =="IV", 3, NA)))),
         Stage_cat=factor(Stage_cat, levels=0:3, labels=c ("StageI", "StageII", "StageIII", "StageIV")),
         #Uninsured as reference
         Insur_cat = ifelse(Insur == "Uninsured",0,
                            ifelse(Insur == "Any Medicaid" ,1,
                                   ifelse(Insur == "Insured"
                                          |Insur == "Insured/No specifics", 2, NA))),
         Insur_cat=factor(Insur_cat, levels=0:2, labels=c ("Uninsured", "Medicaid", "Insured"))
           )
#remove missing data
Data1 <- Data %>%
  filter(First_cat!="No"&!is.na(Stage_cat)&!is.na(Insur_cat)&!is.na(Black_cat)&!is.na(Age_num)&!is.na(Male_cat))

attach(Data1)

Stage_cat_re <- relevel(Stage_cat, ref = "StageIV")
##Execute a multinomial and ordinal regression with insurance as independent variable and demographics as covariates

#multinomial model
mod <- multinom(Stage_cat_re ~ Insur_cat + Age_num + Male_cat + Black_cat)
summary(mod)

#ordinal model
mod1 <- polr(Stage_cat_re ~ Insur_cat + Age_num + Male_cat + Black_cat, Hess=TRUE)
summary(mod1)

#compare models by likelihood ratio test
lrtest(mod, mod1)

#by hand to get actual p-value
dif<-abs(mod$deviance-mod1$deviance)
dif
pchisq(dif,df=18-8,lower.tail=FALSE)
  • What does the model predict?
    • The log odds of being in a category higher than J relative to category J or lower given independent variables
  • How do we interpret the results?
    • The odds of being in a category higher than J relative to category J is X times higher/lower given independent variables.
    • Example of high, medium, low steps walked per week (dependent variable) in association with high, medium, low BMI. The odds of reporting walking a high/medium number of steps per week relative to a low number of steps per week is 2 times higher for those with low BMI vs. high BMI (OR = 2.0).

Poisson regression

  • \(/u\) is known as the rate and is the expected number of times that an event has occurred per unit of time.
  • Model assumptions:
    • Conditional variance equals the conditional mean
  • Dependent variable: Count
  • Independent variables: Continuous or categorical
  • Measure of association: Incidence rate ratio (IRR) (exp of \(\beta\) from the model)
  • How do we interpret the results? For a unit change in x, the expected count/incidence rate changes by a factor of exp(\(\beta\)), other things being equal.
    • Example of incidence of children by age. For every one year increase in age, the incidence rate of children increases by 2% or by a factor of 1.02.
  • What to watch out for: Overdispersion, lots of 0’s (zero inflated), no 0’s (zero truncated)

Negative Binomial regression

  • Use when there is overdispersion
  • Model assumptions:
    • Conditional variance equals the conditional mean plus an overdispersion parameter (alpha) multiplied by a quadratic term of the mean
  • Dependent variable: Count
  • Independent variables: Continuous or categorical
  • Measure of association: Incidence rate ratio (IRR) (exp of \(\beta\) from the model)
  • How do we interpret the results? Same as Poisson
  • What to watch out for: lots of 0 count responses (zero inflated), no 0 count responses (zero truncated)
